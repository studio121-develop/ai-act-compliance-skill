# Deployer Obligations & Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment

If you are a B2B SaaS provider, your customers are almost certainly "deployers" under the
AI Act. That means they have their own legal obligations — and they will look to you to
help them meet those obligations. This document covers everything deployers must do, with
special attention to the Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (FRIA), which is the
deployer-side equivalent of the provider's conformity assessment.

---

## Art. 26: Full Deployer Obligations for High-Risk Systems

A deployer is any natural or legal person that uses an AI system under their authority in
a professional capacity (Art. 3(4)). "Professional capacity" excludes purely personal use,
but includes essentially all business use.

### Core obligations:

**1. Use the system as intended (Art. 26(1))**
Deployers must use the AI system in accordance with the provider's instructions of use.
Deviating from the intended purpose risks triggering Art. 25 (becoming a new provider).

**2. Assign competent human oversight (Art. 26(2))**
Human oversight under Art. 14 must be assigned to natural persons who have:
- The necessary competence, training, and authority to fulfill that function
- The ability to properly understand the system's capabilities and limitations
- The ability to correctly interpret outputs and decide when to override

Recital 72 emphasizes that human oversight is not merely nominal. The person must have
genuine ability to intervene.

**3. Ensure input data relevance (Art. 26(4))**
Where the deployer exercises control over the input data, they must ensure it is relevant
and sufficiently representative for the system's intended purpose. This is particularly
important for systems trained or tuned on provider-supplied data but fed deployer-specific
inputs at runtime.

**4. Monitor the system (Art. 26(5))**
Deployers must monitor the AI system's operation on the basis of the instructions of use.
When they have reason to consider that use in accordance with the instructions may result
in a risk to health, safety, or fundamental rights, they must:
- **Inform the provider or distributor without undue delay**
- **Suspend use of the system** until the issue is resolved
- **Report serious incidents** to the market surveillance authority immediately

**5. Retain automatically generated logs (Art. 26(6))**
Logs generated by the high-risk system must be kept for a minimum of **6 months**, unless
provided otherwise by Union or national law. This intersects with GDPR data minimization
(Art. 5(1)(c) GDPR) — you may need to balance retention with data protection obligations.

**6. Inform workers and their representatives (Art. 26(7))**
Before putting a high-risk AI system into use in the workplace, deployers must inform
workers' representatives and affected workers that they will be subject to the system's
use. This applies to systems listed in Annex III, point 4 (employment, workers management).

**7. Transparency to affected persons (Art. 26(8))**
Deployers of high-risk systems that make decisions about natural persons must inform those
persons that they are subject to the use of a high-risk AI system. For systems used in
the areas listed in Annex III (e.g., creditworthiness, insurance, employment), this
information must be provided before the system is used on them.

**8. Conduct a FRIA when required (Art. 26(9))**
See the detailed section below.

**9. Cooperate with authorities (Art. 26(10))**
Deployers must cooperate with relevant competent authorities and provide information and
documentation on request.

## Art. 27: Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (FRIA)

### When is a FRIA required?

A FRIA is mandatory before first putting a high-risk AI system into use when the deployer
is (Art. 27(1)):

- A **body governed by public law** (government agencies, public authorities)
- A **private entity providing public services** (utilities, healthcare, education)
- A deployer of systems referred to in **Annex III, points 5(b) and 5(c)** (life and
  health insurance, creditworthiness assessment)

Recital 96 explains the rationale: these contexts involve decisions with significant
impact on people's lives, and fundamental rights scrutiny is therefore essential.

Even if not legally required, conducting a FRIA is **strongly recommended** for any
deployer of high-risk AI systems. It demonstrates due diligence, and market surveillance
authorities will look favorably on it.

### What must a FRIA contain?

Under Art. 27(2), the FRIA must include:

1. **A description of the deployer's processes** in which the AI system will be used,
   in line with its intended purpose
2. **The period and frequency** of the system's intended use
3. **The categories of natural persons and groups** likely to be affected
4. **The specific risks of harm** likely to impact the identified categories, taking into
   account information provided by the provider under Art. 13
5. **A description of the implementation of human oversight measures**, in accordance
   with the instructions of use
6. **The measures to be taken** where those risks materialize, including arrangements
   for internal governance and complaint mechanisms
7. **For point 5(b) and 5(c) of Annex III** deployers: the date and results of the FRIA
   under Art. 27(3)

### Relationship to GDPR DPIA (Art. 35 GDPR)

Art. 27(4) explicitly addresses the intersection: where a DPIA is already required under
GDPR Art. 35, the FRIA may be conducted **jointly** with the DPIA. They cover
complementary ground:

| DPIA (GDPR Art. 35) | FRIA (AI Act Art. 27) |
|---------------------|----------------------|
| Focuses on data protection risks | Focuses on all fundamental rights |
| Required for high-risk processing | Required for specific deployer categories |
| Must assess necessity and proportionality | Must assess impact on affected groups |
| Supervised by DPO | No specific officer designated |
| Filed internally (available to DPA) | Notified to market surveillance authority |

Practical recommendation: if you are already doing a DPIA, extend it to cover the FRIA
requirements. The marginal effort is small and the compliance gain is significant.

## Art. 26(5): Obligation to Notify Authorities

This deserves its own section because it is easy to overlook and the consequences of
missing it are severe.

When a deployer identifies a **serious incident** (Art. 3(49)), they must:

1. Immediately inform the provider (and distributor, if applicable)
2. Report the serious incident to the **market surveillance authority** of the Member
   State where the incident occurred
3. Suspend use of the system if continuing poses a risk

A "serious incident" includes (Art. 3(49)):
- Death or serious damage to health
- Serious and irreversible disruption of critical infrastructure management
- Breach of fundamental rights obligations under Union law

Deployers must also cooperate with the provider's post-market monitoring activities
(Art. 72).

1. AI SYSTEM IDENTIFICATION
   System Name: [NAME]
   Provider: [PROVIDER NAME AND CONTACT]
   Version/Release: [VERSION]
   Registration Number (EU Database): [IF AVAILABLE]
   Risk Classification: [HIGH-RISK — specify Annex III category]

2. DEPLOYER INFORMATION
   Organization Name: [NAME]
   Organization Type: [Public body / Private entity providing public services / Other]
   Contact Person: [NAME, ROLE, EMAIL]
   Legal Basis for Use: [Contractual necessity / Legal obligation / Legitimate interest / etc.]

   Intended Purpose: [DESCRIBE how the system will be used, matching the provider's
   stated intended purpose under Art. 6]

   Deployment Context: [DESCRIBE the organizational process into which the system is
   integrated — e.g., "Used as a first-pass screening tool for insurance claims,
   with human review of all flagged cases"]

   Integration Points: [DESCRIBE how the system connects to existing processes,
   data sources, and decision flows]

   Risk 6.1: [TITLE]
   - Fundamental Right(s) Affected: [e.g., non-discrimination (Art. 21 CFR),

   - Description: [HOW this risk may materialize]
   - Likelihood: [Low / Medium / High]
   - Severity: [Low / Medium / High]
   - Affected Groups: [Which categories from Section 5]
   - Provider-Identified: [Yes/No — was this risk noted in the provider's
     instructions of use?]

   Risk 6.2: [TITLE]
   [Repeat structure]

   [Add as many risks as identified]

7. HUMAN OVERSIGHT MEASURES (Art. 27(2)(e))
   Oversight Model: [e.g., Human-in-the-loop / Human-on-the-loop / Human-in-command]

   Designated Oversight Person(s): [NAME(S), ROLE(S), QUALIFICATIONS]

   Training Provided: [DESCRIBE training on the system's capabilities, limitations,
   and override procedures]

   Override Mechanism: [DESCRIBE how the human overseer can intervene, override,
   or reverse decisions]

   Escalation Procedure: [DESCRIBE when and how decisions are escalated]

8. MITIGATION MEASURES (Art. 27(2)(f))
   For each risk in Section 6:

   Mitigation for Risk 6.1:
   - Technical Measures: [e.g., confidence thresholds, bias monitoring]
   - Organizational Measures: [e.g., mandatory human review, sampling audits]
   - Complaint Mechanism: [HOW affected persons can challenge decisions]
   - Redress Procedure: [HOW affected persons can seek remediation]

   [Repeat for each risk]

9. INTERNAL GOVERNANCE
   Responsible Department: [NAME]
   Oversight Committee: [IF APPLICABLE]
   Reporting Cadence: [HOW OFTEN this assessment is reviewed]

## Practical Guidance: Your Customers Are Deployers

If you are a B2B SaaS provider of a high-risk AI system, your customers need the
following from you to meet their deployer obligations:

### What to provide in your product:
- **Clear instructions of use** (Art. 13) — not just API docs, but compliance-oriented
  documentation specifying intended purpose, limitations, and human oversight requirements
- **Automatic log generation and export** — deployers must retain logs for 6 months;
  give them an easy way to do this
- **Human oversight tooling** — dashboards, override buttons, explanation displays
- **Input data quality guidance** — tell deployers what data quality you expect
- **Monitoring capabilities** — alerts, performance drift detection, anomaly flags

### What to provide as documentation:
- Information needed for the deployer's FRIA (risk descriptions, affected categories,
  known limitations)
- Summary of your conformity assessment results
- Your contact information for incident reporting
- Update and patch notification procedure
- Clear scope of intended purpose (so deployers know where the boundary is)

### What to put in your contracts:
- Deployer's obligation to use the system within its intended purpose
- Deployer's obligation to implement human oversight as specified
- Deployer's obligation to report incidents to you
- Your obligation to provide FRIA-supporting information
- Mutual cooperation for market surveillance requests

## Key Takeaways

1. **Deployers are not passive users.** They have direct legal obligations under the
   AI Act, including monitoring, logging, transparency, and FRIA.
2. **The FRIA is the deployer's key compliance artifact.** For required categories, it
   must be completed before the system is used.
3. **FRIA and DPIA can be combined.** If you already do GDPR DPIAs, extend them — do
   not create parallel processes.
4. **Provider and deployer obligations are complementary.** Providers must equip deployers
   to comply. Deployers must actually use what providers give them.
5. **Incident reporting flows both ways.** Deployers report to providers and to
   authorities. Build this into your operational procedures, not just your legal docs.
