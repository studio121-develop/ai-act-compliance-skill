# Regulatory Sandboxes (Arts. 57-62)

> **Disclaimer**: This document provides general guidance — not legal advice. See [LEGAL_NOTICE.md](/LEGAL_NOTICE.md) for full terms.

Regulatory sandboxes are controlled environments set up by national competent authorities
where you can develop, train, validate, and test AI systems under regulatory supervision
before placing them on the market. Think of them as a compliance rehearsal with a safety
net — the regulator is in the room with you, providing guidance instead of penalties.

Even if your system is not high-risk, sandboxes offer a genuine advantage: real-time
regulatory feedback, a compliance head start, and a signal to the market that you take
AI governance seriously.

---

## Art. 57: Establishment of Sandboxes by Member States

Each Member State must ensure that its national competent authorities establish at least
one AI regulatory sandbox at national level, operational by **2 August 2026**
(Art. 57(1)). Sandboxes may also be established at regional or local level.

Key design principles (Art. 57(1)-(4)):

- Sandboxes must **facilitate the development and testing** of innovative AI systems
  under strict regulatory oversight before placement on the market
- They must **enhance legal certainty** and support the sharing of best practices
- The European AI Office may establish a single sandbox for testing GPAI models
  (Art. 57(5))
- Member States must coordinate to avoid fragmentation and ensure interoperability of
  sandbox frameworks (Art. 57(10))

Recital 138 emphasizes that sandboxes should not be understood as merely permissive — they
are structured environments with oversight, not free-for-all testing zones.

### Joint Sandboxes
Two or more Member States may establish joint regulatory sandboxes (Art. 57(8)). The
participating authorities must agree on rules for operation, including supervisory
responsibilities and data protection oversight.

## Art. 58: Conditions for Participation

### Who can apply?

Any prospective provider or prospective deployer can apply to participate in a sandbox
(Art. 58(1)). This includes:

- Startups and SMEs (with priority access — see Art. 62)
- Established companies developing new AI systems
- Research institutions
- Companies from non-EU countries (subject to the sandbox rules of the host state)

### Application requirements (Art. 58(1)):

An application must include at minimum:
- A description of the AI system and its intended purpose
- An innovation and testing plan
- A supervision and exit plan
- The expected timeline

### Admission criteria (Art. 58(2)):

The competent authority shall admit applicants that demonstrate:

1. The AI system represents a **genuine innovation** or a significant improvement
2. There is no existing Union or national law that clearly allows the proposed activity
   without a sandbox (i.e., the sandbox adds regulatory clarity)
3. The applicant has a credible **testing plan** that can be implemented during the
   sandbox period
4. The applicant demonstrates a credible plan for **market deployment** after the sandbox
5. Participation does not pose **unacceptable risks** to health, safety, or fundamental
   rights

### Sandbox agreement (Art. 58(4)):

Upon admission, the competent authority and the participant agree on a **sandbox plan**
that sets out:
- The objectives and expected outcomes
- The conditions for participation (including geographic, temporal, and use-case scope)
- The modalities for monitoring and supervision
- The exit strategy and conditions for graduation
- The consequences of non-compliance with sandbox conditions

### Duration:

The AI Act does not set a fixed duration. Based on early implementations, expect sandbox
periods of **12 to 24 months**, with the possibility of extension.

## Art. 59: Processing of Personal Data in Sandboxes

This article bridges the AI Act and GDPR — a critical intersection for any AI system
that processes personal data.

Personal data lawfully collected for other purposes may be processed in the sandbox
**solely for the purpose of developing, training, and testing** the AI system, provided
that all of the following conditions are met (Art. 59(1)):

1. The AI system is developed for safeguarding **substantial public interest** in one
   or more of the areas listed (public safety, environment, energy, transport safety,
   financial system integrity, healthcare)
2. The data processed is necessary for **compliance with one or more AI Act
   requirements** (e.g., bias detection under Art. 10)
3. Effective **monitoring mechanisms** exist to identify risks to fundamental rights
4. Personal data are processed in a **functionally separate, isolated, and protected**
   environment under the participant's control
5. Personal data are **not transmitted, transferred, or otherwise accessed** by other
   parties
6. Processing does not lead to measures or decisions **affecting data subjects**
7. Personal data are **deleted** after the sandbox period or when consent is withdrawn
8. Processing **logs** are kept for the sandbox duration plus a reasonable period

Art. 59(2) requires that a DPIA (GDPR Art. 35) is carried out before processing begins.

Recital 140 clarifies that this article does not create a new legal basis for processing
personal data. It permits reuse of lawfully collected data under controlled conditions
within the sandbox — a significant practical benefit for AI training.

## Art. 60: Testing in Real-World Conditions for High-Risk Systems

High-risk AI systems can be tested in real-world conditions outside the sandbox, subject
to strict safeguards (Art. 60(1)):

### Conditions for real-world testing:

1. A **real-world testing plan** has been drawn up and submitted to the market
   surveillance authority (Art. 60(4))
2. The market surveillance authority has **approved** the plan or has not objected within
   30 working days (Art. 60(4))
3. The testing occurs for a **limited period** and in a limited geographic area
4. An appropriate **human oversight** mechanism is in place
5. Adequate **safeguards** protect the rights of persons subject to the testing
6. The testing does not have a **negative effect** on the tested persons
7. Results of the testing are **reversible** or can be disregarded
8. Tested persons have given **informed consent** (Art. 61)

### What the testing plan must contain (Art. 60(4)):

- Information identifying the provider/prospective provider and participants
- The testing protocol, including objectives, methodology, and evaluation criteria
- Risk assessment and mitigation measures
- Description of the testing environment and duration
- Human oversight modalities
- Monitoring, data recording, and incident reporting arrangements
- Insurance or indemnification coverage

### Testing by deployers:

Deployers can also conduct real-world testing, but only with the provider's authorization
and under conditions that do not deviate from the system's intended purpose (Art. 60(6)).

## Art. 61: Informed Consent for Real-World Testing

Persons subject to real-world testing must be informed and give their **free, specific,
informed, and unambiguous consent** before the testing takes place (Art. 61(1)).

The information must include at minimum:
- The nature and purpose of the real-world testing
- The conditions under which the testing will be conducted
- The rights of the tested person (including the right to withdraw at any time)
- How to exercise those rights
- Contact details for complaints

Art. 61(2) provides exceptions for law enforcement, border management, and asylum/
immigration contexts under strict additional conditions — these are narrowly drawn and
require judicial or administrative authorization.

## Art. 62: SME-Specific Measures

The AI Act explicitly recognizes that SMEs and startups face disproportionate compliance
burdens and includes specific support measures (Art. 62(1)):

### Priority access:
- SMEs (including startups) shall be given **priority access** to regulatory sandboxes,
  provided they meet the eligibility conditions (Art. 62(1)(a))

### Reduced fees:
- Conformity assessment fees charged by notified bodies must take into account the
  specific interests and needs of SMEs, **reducing fees proportionately** to their size,
  market share, and economic situation (Art. 62(1)(b))

### Simplified documentation:
- The AI Office and Member States must develop **simplified compliance pathways** and
  provide guidance materials targeted at SME needs (Art. 62(2))

### Awareness and training:
- Member States must undertake actions to provide SMEs with **awareness, education,
  and training** on AI Act obligations (Art. 62(3))

### Dedicated communication channels:
- National competent authorities must establish **dedicated communication channels**
  for SMEs and other innovators (Art. 62(1)(c))

Recital 144 makes the policy intent clear: the AI Act should not create a barrier to
innovation for smaller players. Sandboxes and reduced fees are meant to level the playing
field.

## Current Sandbox Availability by Country

As of early 2026, the sandbox landscape is still developing. The deadline for all Member
States to have at least one operational sandbox is **2 August 2026**.

### Operational or launched:

| Country | Status | Notes |
|---------|--------|-------|
| **Spain** | Operational (first in EU) | Launched pilot in 2022 under AESIA; expanded under AI Act framework in 2025. Focus on healthcare, financial services, and public administration. |
| **France** | Launched | CNIL-coordinated sandbox with AI and data protection focus; strong GDPR-DPIA integration. |
| **Netherlands** | Launched | Operated by Autoriteit Persoonsgegevens (AP); emphasizes bias testing and algorithmic transparency. |
| **Germany** | In preparation | BNetzA (Federal Network Agency) leading; expected multiple regional sandboxes. |
| **Italy** | In preparation | AgID coordinating; focus on public sector AI. |
| **Finland** | Launched | Traficom-operated; emphasis on AI in transport and communications. |
| **Denmark** | In preparation | Digitaliseringsstyrelsen (Agency for Digital Government) leading. |
| **Malta** | Launched | MDIA (Malta Digital Innovation Authority); small-scale, startup-friendly. |

### European AI Office sandbox:
The European AI Office has indicated it will establish a sandbox specifically for GPAI
model evaluation under Art. 57(5). Details are expected in the second half of 2026.

Note: Check the AI Pact portal (https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/ai-pact)
and the AI Office website for the most current sandbox listings.

## Practical Guide: How to Apply

### Step 1: Determine the right sandbox
- Identify which Member State sandbox best matches your use case
- Consider language, market focus, and sectoral specialization
- If you serve multiple EU markets, consider the sandbox of your primary market

### Step 2: Prepare your application

At minimum, prepare:
- **System description**: What your AI system does, how it works, what data it uses
- **Innovation statement**: Why this system represents genuine innovation or improvement
- **Testing plan**: Specific hypotheses to test, metrics, timeline
- **Risk assessment**: Known and anticipated risks, mitigation strategies
- **Market deployment plan**: How you will move from sandbox to market
- **Team credentials**: Who will be involved, their qualifications
- **Resource commitment**: Dedicated staff, estimated time investment

### Step 3: Submit and engage
- Apply through the national competent authority's portal or contact point
- Expect a review period of **4 to 12 weeks** depending on the Member State
- Be prepared for iterative feedback — authorities often request clarification

### Step 4: During the sandbox
- Maintain close communication with your supervisory contact
- Document everything — testing results, modifications, incidents
- Use the opportunity to stress-test your compliance documentation
- Request explicit regulatory guidance on ambiguous compliance questions

### Step 5: Exit and graduation
- Compile your sandbox report, including outcomes and lessons learned
- Update your technical documentation and risk management based on findings
- The competent authority may issue a **sandbox outcome report** that can support your
  conformity assessment

### Timeline expectations:
- Application to admission: **1-3 months**
- Sandbox period: **12-24 months**
- Post-sandbox transition: **3-6 months**
- Total from application to market readiness: **18-30 months**

## Why You Should Care Even If You Are Not High-Risk

Sandboxes are not exclusively for high-risk AI systems. Here is why participating
matters even for lower-risk products:

1. **Regulatory certainty**: Get real-time guidance on whether your system is classified
   correctly. If the authority confirms you are not high-risk, that is valuable
   documentation.

2. **Compliance head start**: Even limited-risk and minimal-risk systems benefit from
   structured compliance processes. A sandbox gives you a framework.

3. **Market signal**: Participating in a sandbox demonstrates regulatory maturity to
   enterprise customers, especially in regulated sectors (finance, healthcare, insurance).

4. **Access to expertise**: Sandbox supervisors are among the most knowledgeable people
   on AI Act implementation. Their feedback is invaluable.

5. **Network effects**: Sandboxes typically bring together multiple participants.
   You gain access to peer companies, regulators, and potentially customers.

6. **SME advantages**: Reduced fees, priority access, and simplified procedures under
   Art. 62 make sandboxes particularly attractive for startups and small companies.

7. **Preparation for reclassification**: If AI Act classifications evolve (through
   delegated acts or updated Annex III), having sandbox experience positions you well.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Every Member State must have a sandbox by August 2026.** Availability is expanding
   rapidly — check the current state for your target market.
2. **SMEs get priority access and reduced fees.** If you qualify, apply early.
3. **Sandboxes solve the data problem.** Art. 59 allows reuse of personal data under
   controlled conditions, which is otherwise difficult under GDPR.
4. **Real-world testing of high-risk systems** has a structured path (Art. 60-61) but
   requires informed consent and authority approval.
5. **The compliance insight alone is worth it.** Even if you graduate from a sandbox
   and decide your system is not high-risk, the regulatory guidance you receive is
   documentation gold.
